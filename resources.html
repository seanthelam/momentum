What is an REU?

REU stands for Research Experience for Undergraduates. These are summer programs funded by the National Science Foundation (NSF) that give undergraduate students hands-on research experience at universities and research institutions across the country.

What to Expect

  • Duration: Typically 8-10 weeks during summer
  • Stipend: Usually $5,000-$7,000 plus housing and sometimes travel support
  • Experience: Work on a real research project with a faculty mentor
  • Community: Meet other undergraduate researchers from across the country
  • Professional development: Workshops on graduate school, careers, scientific writing
  • Final presentation: Present your research findings at end of program

Why Participate?

  • Explore whether you enjoy research and want to pursue graduate school
  • Build your CV with research experience (essential for grad school applications)
  • Get a strong letter of recommendation from a research mentor
  • Learn new experimental or computational skills
  • Experience life at a different university or research lab
  • Network with faculty, grad students, and peers in your field
  • Earn money while gaining valuable experience

Who Should Apply?

REUs are open to all undergraduate students, though most participants are sophomores and juniors. You don't need extensive research experience‚Äîmany programs specifically welcome first-time researchers. You should apply if:

  • You're curious about a subfield of physics
  • You're considering graduate school (or want to explore if it's right for you)
  • You want hands-on experience beyond classroom learning
  • You're a sophomore or junior with at least Modern Physics completed
  • You're eager to learn new skills and work on challenging problems

Writing a Strong Personal Statement

Your personal statement is your chance to show programs who you are, what drives your interest in physics, and why you're a great fit for their REU. Here's how to craft a compelling statement.

What Programs Want to See

  • Genuine enthusiasm for the research area
  • Specific knowledge about the program and why it fits your interests
  • Relevant experience (coursework, projects, research)
  • Intellectual curiosity and ability to think critically
  • Clear goals for what you hope to learn
  • Strong writing skills and attention to detail

Structure (Aim for ~500-750 words)

Paragraph 1: Opening Hook (3-4 sentences)

Start with a compelling moment that sparked your interest in physics or the specific research area. This should be specific and personal‚Äînot generic.

Example: "During my Modern Physics course, I became fascinated by quantum entanglement when we discussed the EPR paradox. The idea that particles could be instantaneously correlated across vast distances defied my classical intuition and motivated me to explore quantum information theory further."

Paragraph 2: Your Background (4-6 sentences)

Discuss relevant coursework, skills, and any prior research or projects. Connect these experiences to the REU program's focus.

Example: "I've completed coursework in Quantum Mechanics and Electromagnetic Theory, and developed strong computational skills through my Computational Physics class. Last semester, I worked with Professor Smith on modeling quantum systems using Python, which strengthened my programming abilities and introduced me to numerical methods in quantum mechanics."

Paragraph 3: Why This Program? (4-6 sentences)

Show you've researched the program. Mention specific faculty, projects, or facilities that interest you. Be specific!

Example: "I'm particularly excited about the opportunity to work with Professor Johnson on quantum computing implementations. Her recent paper on superconducting qubits aligns perfectly with my interest in experimental quantum information. Additionally, your program's emphasis on collaborative research and professional development workshops would help me develop both technical and communication skills."

Paragraph 4: Your Goals & Closing (3-4 sentences)

Explain what you hope to gain from the experience and how it fits into your broader goals.

Example: "Through this REU, I hope to gain hands-on experience with quantum hardware and determine whether experimental or theoretical research better suits my interests. I'm considering graduate school in physics, and this program would provide invaluable exposure to cutting-edge research while helping me refine my career goals."

Do's and Don'ts

‚úì Do

  • Tailor each statement to the specific program
  • Be specific about research interests
  • Show you've researched the program
  • Mention specific faculty you'd like to work with
  • Be authentic and let your personality show
  • Proofread multiple times
  • Get feedback from professors or peers

‚úó Don't

  • Use the same generic statement for every program
  • Just list your coursework or GPA
  • Be vague about your interests
  • Oversell yourself or exaggerate experience
  • Use flowery or overly formal language
  • Make it only about the stipend/location
  • Submit without proofreading

How to Contact Professors About Research

Reaching out to faculty about research opportunities‚Äîwhether for summer programs or academic-year positions‚Äîrequires professionalism and preparation. Here's how to write effective emails.

Before You Email

  • Read the professor's recent papers or check their website
  • Look for any posted instructions about contacting them for research
  • Make sure their research genuinely interests you
  • Check if they're accepting students (some faculty websites specify)
  • Identify specific projects or papers that excite you

Email Template

Subject Line

Prospective Summer Research Student - [Your Name]

or

Research Opportunity in [Research Area]

Email Body

Dear Professor [Last Name],

I am a [year] physics major at Colorado College, and I am writing to inquire about potential research opportunities in your group for summer 2026. I recently read your paper on [specific topic] and was fascinated by [specific aspect that interested you].

I have completed coursework in [relevant courses] and have experience with [relevant skills‚Äîprogramming languages, lab techniques, etc.]. [Optional: Mention any relevant research experience or projects.] I am particularly interested in [specific aspect of their research] because [genuine reason].

Would you be available for a brief meeting to discuss potential projects? I have attached my CV and transcript for your reference. I am also applying to the [NSF REU program name, if applicable] and would be thrilled to work with your group through that program.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Best regards,
[Your Full Name]
[Your Email]
[Your Phone Number]
Colorado College, Class of [Year]

Email Tips

  • Be concise: Keep it to 3-4 short paragraphs
  • Be specific: Mention actual papers or projects
  • Attach your CV: Make it easy for them to evaluate you
  • Follow up: If no response in 1-2 weeks, send a polite follow-up
  • Professional email address: Use firstname.lastname@coloradocollege.edu
  • Time it right: Email early (November-December for summer positions)

  • Generic emails that could be sent to anyone
  • Not researching the professor's work beforehand
  • Asking them to explain their research (you should already know)
  • Very long emails (professors are busy!)
  • Typos or grammatical errors (proofread!)
  • Demanding or entitled tone

Requesting Letters of Recommendation

Strong letters of recommendation are crucial for REU and graduate school applications. Here's how to ask for them effectively and give your recommenders what they need to write strong letters.

Who to Ask

  • Professors who know you well: From classes where you excelled, participated actively, or did a project
  • Research mentors: Faculty you've done research with (these are ideal)
  • Lab instructors: If you've done well in lab courses and interacted significantly
  • NOT: TAs, graduate students, or professors you've never spoken to

When to Ask

  • At least 3-4 weeks before the deadline (more time is even better)
  • Earlier in the semester is better than during finals
  • Give extra time if they need to write multiple letters
  • Account for breaks and holidays

How to Ask (In Person First!)

  1. Ask in person or via video call if possible: "Would you be willing to write me a strong letter of recommendation for REU programs?"
  2. Give them an out: "I understand if you're too busy or don't feel you know my work well enough."
  3. If they agree: Follow up with an email containing all materials (see below)

What to Provide Your Recommender

Following Up

  • Send reminders: One week before deadline: "Just a friendly reminder that the deadline for [program] is [date]."
  • Track submissions: Keep track of which letters have been submitted
  • Thank them: After everything is submitted, send a thank-you email
  • Update them: Let them know the outcomes when you hear back!

General Application Tips

Do's ‚úì

  • Start early (December/January for summer programs)
  • Tailor each application to the specific program
  • Highlight relevant coursework and skills
  • Show genuine enthusiasm for the research area
  • Proofread everything multiple times
  • Ask for rec letters at least 3 weeks in advance
  • Apply to a range of programs (8-12 is typical)

Don'ts ‚úó

  • Don't use the same personal statement for every application
  • Don't wait until the deadline to ask for letters
  • Don't oversell skills you don't have
  • Don't forget to follow application instructions
  • Don't apply only to "prestigious" programs
  • Don't get discouraged by rejections‚Äîapply broadly!
  • Don't forget to thank your recommenders
