Graduate School Application Timeline

A comprehensive guide to applying to physics PhD programs. Most applications are due in December of your senior year.

üìå Key Dates at a Glance

  • Sophomore/Junior Year: Build research experience (REUs)
  • Summer before Senior Year: Final REU, begin planning
  • September-October: Take GRE Physics (optional/not required at most schools now), request letters
  • November: Write and revise statements of purpose
  • December 1-15: Most application deadlines
  • January-March: Interview season and visit days
  • April 15: Decision deadline (Council of Graduate Schools resolution)

Overview

Applying to physics PhD programs is a major undertaking that requires planning, research, and strong preparation. Most programs are fully funded (tuition + stipend), so you're applying to be both a student and a researcher. The timeline below will help you stay organized through the year-long process.

üìä Typical Application Components

  • Statement of Purpose (~2 pages)
  • 3 Letters of Recommendation
  • Transcripts
  • GRE General (some schools)
  • GRE Physics Subject Test (some schools)
  • CV/Resume

üéØ What Programs Look For

  • Research experience (essential!)
  • Strong physics coursework
  • Clear research interests
  • Good fit with faculty
  • Potential for independent research
  • Strong letters from research mentors

üìà Application Strategy

  • Apply to 8-12 programs
  • Mix of "reach", "match", and "safety"
  • Consider program fit, not just ranking
  • Research specific faculty at each school
  • Geographic and cultural fit matters

Month-by-Month Timeline

Sophomore & Junior Years
üèóÔ∏è Build Your Foundation
  • Complete core physics courses (Quantum Mechanics I & II, Electromagnetic Theory, Classical Mechanics, Statistical Mechanics)
  • Participate in 1-2 summer REUs or research experiences
  • Develop strong relationships with potential letter writers (professors who know your work well)
  • Explore different subfields of physics through courses and research
  • Maintain a strong GPA, especially in physics and math courses
  • Consider joining SPS and attending conferences (APS meetings, regional conferences)
  • Start thinking about what areas of physics excite you most
Summer Before Senior Year (May-August)
üî¨ Final Summer Research
  • Complete your last REU or research experience (ideally at a potential graduate school or with faculty connections)
  • Start making a list of 15-20 schools that interest you
  • Research faculty at these institutions‚Äîread their recent papers
  • Consider taking GRE General Test if required (check school requirements)
  • Begin outlining your statement of purpose
  • Ask your summer research mentor about their grad school experience
  • Network with grad students to learn about their programs
September (Senior Year)
üìã Preparation Phase
  • Finalize list of 8-12 programs to apply to (consider research fit, location, program culture)
  • Register for GRE Physics Subject Test (typically offered in September and October) if necessary
  • Create a spreadsheet tracking all schools, deadlines, requirements
  • Research specific faculty at each program you're applying to
  • Begin drafting your statement of purpose
  • Order transcripts from all institutions attended
  • Update your CV with all research experiences, presentations, coursework
  • Attend grad school info sessions if available
October (Senior Year)
üìù Testing & Letters
  • CRITICAL: Request letters of recommendation (give at least 4-6 weeks before first deadline!)
    • Ask 3 professors who know your research/academic work well
    • Provide them with: CV, statement of purpose draft, list of schools with deadlines, unofficial transcript
    • Include brief notes on why you're applying to each school
  • Take GRE Physics Subject Test (offered in September or October) if necessary
  • Continue drafting and revising statement of purpose
  • Get feedback on your statement from professors, writing center, peers
  • Research each program's specific focus areas and faculty
  • Start creating application portal accounts
November (Senior Year)
‚úçÔ∏è Application Writing
  • Finalize statement of purpose for each school
    • Tailor each statement to specific programs and faculty
    • Explain your research interests and experiences
    • Show why you're a good fit for that specific program
    • Mention 2-3 faculty you'd like to work with
  • Get multiple rounds of feedback on statements
  • Finalize your CV
  • Double-check all application requirements for each school
  • Prepare for any required interviews or pre-application contacts
  • Some programs encourage reaching out to potential advisors‚Äîdo this thoughtfully
  • Check in with recommenders about progress
December (Senior Year)
üì® Submit Applications
  • Most deadlines fall between December 1-15
  • Submit applications at least 2-3 days before deadlines
  • Verify all components are uploaded correctly
  • Ensure recommenders have submitted letters (send gentle reminder 1 week before deadline)
  • Pay application fees (fee waivers available for eligible students)
  • Keep copies of all submitted materials
  • Confirm receipt of applications
  • Take a breath‚Äîyou've done great work!
January-February
‚è∞ Waiting Period & Early Responses
  • Programs begin reviewing applications
  • Some schools may request additional information
  • Early interview invitations may arrive (typically late January onward)
  • Continue with senior year coursework
  • Some early rejections or acceptances may come
  • Stay in touch with your recommenders about any updates
  • Prepare for potential interviews (practice talks, questions about research)
Late February - March
üé§ Interviews & Visit Days
  • Attend virtual or in-person visit weekends (all expenses typically paid by programs)
  • Meet with potential advisors and current graduate students
  • Prepare thoughtful questions about:
    • Research projects and funding
    • Advisor mentoring style
    • Program requirements and timeline
    • Qualifying exams and milestones
    • Graduate student life and culture
    • Placement after graduation
  • Take notes on each program‚Äîthey'll blur together!
  • Assess fit beyond just prestige (culture, location, advisor, research)
  • Talk to multiple grad students, not just those at official events
April
üéâ Decision Time
  • Most programs send final decisions by early-mid April
  • April 15: National decision deadline (CGS Resolution)
    • You don't have to decide before this date
    • Programs can't pressure you to decide earlier
    • Most schools extend this to April 15 for fairness
  • If choosing between offers, consider:
    • Research fit and advisor compatibility
    • Program structure and flexibility
    • Funding and stipend
    • Location and quality of life
    • Career outcomes and alumni network
    • Your gut feeling about the program
  • Accept your chosen offer promptly
  • Decline other offers professionally to open spots for waitlisted students
  • Thank your recommenders and update them on your decision!
  • Celebrate your accomplishment! üéä
Summer After Graduation
üéí Preparation for Grad School
  • Arrange housing and move logistics
  • Complete any required paperwork or onboarding
  • Connect with your future advisor and incoming cohort
  • Review relevant physics topics (quantum, E&M, classical)
  • Brush up on coding skills if needed (Python, C++, MATLAB)
  • Read recent papers from your research group
  • Enjoy your summer‚Äîgrad school is intense!

Additional Advice

‚úÖ Do's

  • Start early and stay organized
  • Research programs and faculty thoroughly
  • Tailor each statement to the specific program
  • Get feedback from multiple people
  • Apply to a range of programs (reach, match, safety)
  • Consider program fit over just rankings
  • Ask grad students honest questions during visits
  • Trust your instincts about advisor fit

‚ùå Don'ts

  • Don't apply only to top-10 schools
  • Don't use generic statements for every school
  • Don't wait until the last minute to request letters
  • Don't neglect to research specific faculty
  • Don't choose based on prestige alone
  • Don't forget to ask current students about culture
  • Don't ignore red flags during visits
  • Don't feel pressure to decide before April 15

üí∞ Funding & Costs

Good news: Most physics PhD programs in the US provide full funding including:

  • Full tuition waiver
  • Stipend ($25,000-$45,000/year depending on location and school)
  • Health insurance

Application costs: Expect to spend $800-$1,500 total on:

  • Application fees (~$75-$100 per school, fee waivers available)
  • GRE General (~$220) - optional
  • GRE Physics Subject Test (~$150) - optional
  • Sending GRE scores (~$27 per school) - optional
  • Transcripts

Note: Many schools have moved away from requiring GRE scores‚Äîcheck individual program requirements.
